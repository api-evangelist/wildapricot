<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>How can nonprofits accept online payments?</strong>&nbsp;</h2>



<p>Nonprofits accept payments through a secure online donation form which is connected to a payment processor.&nbsp;</p>



<p>The two main payment options are:&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Major credit cards</strong>: Donors simply type in their card number and the money zips over through a credit card network. This type of payment processing usually costs a fixed rate plus a percentage of each transaction. </li>



<li><strong>ACH (“Automatic Clearing House”)</strong>: Also known as “direct payments,” ACH processing moves the money directly from a donor’s debit bank account. Think of it as an electronic check! Because it cuts out the credit card verification process, it usually charges you a flat fee. </li>
</ul>



<p>Be sure to have BOTH options available to your donors. That way, anyone can&nbsp;donate&nbsp;any time!&nbsp;</p>



<p>There are also a couple of less common ways to accept donations online, like:&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Digital wallets (Apple Pay, Google Pay) </li>



<li>Venmo / PayPal / Cash App / Zelle (with pros/cons) </li>



<li>Text-to-give &amp; mobile giving </li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Key nonprofit payment processing terms you should know</strong>&nbsp;</h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Payment Gateway: </strong>Tech that securely captures and transmits payment information from the customer to the payment processor. Think of it as the bridge between your website/POS and payment type. </li>



<li><strong>Payment Processor: </strong>The company handling your transaction and communicating between the customer&#8217;s bank and the merchant&#8217;s bank. Think of it as your payment liaison. </li>



<li><strong>Merchant Account: </strong>A special type of bank account for businesses to accept debit and credit card payments. This is where the funds are temporarily held before being transmitted to the business&#8217;s main bank account. When your payment says &#8220;pending&#8221; in your personal account, the funds are in the merchant account. </li>



<li><strong>Aggregator (third-party processor): </strong>A payment provider that allows organizations to accept payments without setting up a merchant account. Common examples are Stripe, PayPal, and Square. </li>



<li><strong>ACH: </strong>An electronic bank-to-bank payment system used for transfers like direct deposits, bill payments, and e-checks. The bonus is lower fees, but you&#8217;ll typically deal with longer processing times. </li>



<li><strong>PCI Compliance: </strong>Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS), these are a set of security requirements that protect cardholder data and reduce fraud. </li>



<li><strong>Tokenization / Encryption: </strong>Another security factor for payments. Tokenization replaces card information with a random &#8220;token&#8221; that can be stored and used for future transactions. Encryption does a similar thing: during payment transmission, the payment data is converted into an unreadable code. </li>



<li><strong>Chargeback: </strong>When a customer disputes a transaction with their bank, the funds are returned to the customer while the payment is investigated.  </li>



<li><strong>Recurring Billing: </strong>An automated payment setup where a customer is charged on a regular schedule for memberships, subscriptions, or installment plans. </li>
</ul>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Be Transparent About Fees and Total Cost</strong>&nbsp;</h3>



<p>Payment processors can include hidden costs beyond the expected payment. These extra fees can vary depending on the form of payment, such as card type (Visa versus Amex), payment method (ACH),&nbsp;currency&nbsp;and more. When choosing a payment processor, be sure to look at:&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Transaction fees:</strong> These will be charged every time a payment is processed and cover bank and card network costs. These are unavoiable if you&#8217;re accepting credit cards. Typically formatted as a percentage of the transaction (2.9%) plus a flat per-transaction fee ($.30) </li>



<li><strong>Monthly fees: </strong>This monthly charge is fixed and will be charged to your business regardless of how many transactions are processed. </li>



<li><strong>Platform fees: </strong>This fee can be confused with transaction fees, but platform fees cover the cost of the processor software. Depending on the software you choose, sometimes donors can cover this fee, allowing your organization to keep the majority of donations. </li>



<li><strong>Chargeback fees: </strong>An additional fee you incur if someone files a dispute with their bank on a payment you process. Typically, this is $15-$25 per incident and too many chargebacks on your business can result in higher processing rates.  </li>



<li><strong>International/currency conversion fees:</strong> If you accept payments from international individuals, you may be charged cross-border fees (.5%-1.5%) or a currency conversion fee (1%-3%).  </li>



<li><strong>Other hidden costs, like support charges: </strong>These could be additional fees or charges that can drastically change your total cost of payment processing. Be on the lookout for fees related to account setup, customer support, hardware, and refunds. </li>
</ul>



<p>For example, a $100 payment made to your organization could look like this:&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Credit card transaction and processing fee (2.9% + $.30) and platform fee: (+1%) </li>



<li>($100 x 3.9%) + $.30 = $4.20 total fees </li>



<li>Your organization receives $95.80  </li>
</ul>



<p>Knowing all the potential fees associated with payment processing software will help you find technology that fits your organization&#8217;s budget. Make sure to ask about all these hidden costs and read the fine print before committing to software.&nbsp;</p>

<p>When individuals donate to your organization, they want to know their contributions are making an impact as soon as possible, and you want to use these funds for your projects&nbsp;immediately. <p>Some payment processors offer instant payout options. This enables you to receive your payments within minutes, rather than days. <h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Three Main Credit Card Processing Options for Nonprofits&nbsp;</strong>&nbsp;</h2>



<p>Most of the time, your organization will accept credit card payments, so&nbsp;it&#8217;s&nbsp;important to be aware of the options available for accepting them. The three main options for accepting credit card payments for nonprofits are through a third-party payment processor, a merchant account, or software that also processes payments.&nbsp;I’ve&nbsp;outlined what you can expect from each of them below.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>1. Third Party Payment Processor&nbsp;</strong>&nbsp;</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Typical Fees</strong>: <a href="http://blog.unibulmerchantservices.com/dedicated-merchant-accounts-vs-third-party-payment-services/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">2.9% + $0.30</a> per transaction. Usually no other fees.  </li>



<li>Customers may recognize the name if you use a widely accepted payment processor (we&#8217;ll share our recommendations later in this article), but there are fewer opportunities to brand it with your identity.  </li>
</ul>



<p>If your nonprofit is expecting a low volume of monthly transactions (less than a hundred), you might want to choose a third-party payment processor.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>Set up is&nbsp;relatively quick, but transaction fees are higher since&nbsp;you’re&nbsp;paying a third party to process transactions via their own merchant account.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Keep in mind that third-party payment processors will limit your ability to accept credit card payments in person.&nbsp;You&#8217;ll&nbsp;need some sort of hardware, like a card reader or POS, for these transactions.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>2. Merchant Account&nbsp;</strong>&nbsp;</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Typical Fees</strong>: <a href="http://blog.unibulmerchantservices.com/dedicated-merchant-accounts-vs-third-party-payment-services/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">2.15% + $0.25</a> per transaction. Set up/monthly fees vary.  </li>



<li>Merchant accounts enable you to accept offline payments and donations via a hand-held credit card reader.  </li>
</ul>



<p>For organizations with&nbsp;a very high&nbsp;volume&nbsp;of in person&nbsp;monthly transactions (hundreds or more), it might be easier to choose a merchant account. While transaction fees for a Merchant Account are typically lower than a&nbsp;Third Party&nbsp;Payment Processor, it takes a little more time to set up your account.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>With a merchant account,&nbsp;you&#8217;ll&nbsp;need a credit card processor to accept offline payments. Your options will be a credit card terminal, a credit card&nbsp;swiper&nbsp;or manually enter payments via a virtual terminal.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong><em>Credit Card Terminals vs. Credit Card Swipers vs. Virtual Terminals&nbsp;</em></strong>&nbsp;</h3>



<p>After setting up your merchant account, the payment processing solution you choose can help you get the&nbsp;appropriate hardware. But of the 3 types of credit card processors, which is right for your nonprofit?&nbsp;</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Credit Card Terminals&nbsp;</strong>&nbsp;</h4>



<p>A credit card terminal is the same tech&nbsp;you&#8217;re&nbsp;used to using at most retail stores.&nbsp;It&#8217;s&nbsp;a simple card reader that customers can insert, swipe or tap their card against&nbsp;to pay.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>Models can be wired or wireless – both have their pros and cons. Wired terminals are much more likely to remain connected even in the case of an internet outage,&nbsp;whereas&nbsp;wireless terminals are much lighter and more portable. Unfortunately, both can be more difficult to connect to other software providers, like fundraising software, and are more expensive than a card swiper.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Credit Card Swiper&nbsp;</strong>&nbsp;</h4>



<p>With that in mind, you may want to consider using a credit card swiper. These lightweight devices can easily be attached to a phone or tablet. Simply download the app associated with your payment processor,&nbsp;purchase&nbsp;a card swiper from&nbsp;them&nbsp;and&nbsp;you’re&nbsp;good to go!&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Virtual Terminal&nbsp;</strong>&nbsp;</h4>



<p>Finally, if you&nbsp;don’t&nbsp;mind entering credit card numbers manually, you can also simply use&nbsp;<a href="https://www.merchantmaverick.com/virtual-terminal/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">a virtual terminal</a>. This is&nbsp;basically the&nbsp;same as the donation page on your website — it allows you to enter the card details into a form and then charge them online.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>However, if you need to process a lot of transactions, it can be incredibly time-consuming. Further, individuals may not feel comfortable letting someone else type in their CVV and credit card number.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>3. Credit Card Processing Through Software&nbsp;</strong>&nbsp;</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Typical Fees</strong>: Vary based on the software you select. Payment processing fees will be comparable to those for a merchant account.  </li>



<li>A more robust solution, credit card processing through software is when your technology includes payment processing along with their other features. </li>
</ul>

<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>VPNs (virtual private networks)</li>



<li>Payment gateways</li>



<li>IP Blocking</li>
</ul>



<p>Again, a bunch of these are detailed in PCI compliance requirements—if your processor says it&#8217;s PCI-compliant at the level you need, you&#8217;re good to go. Remember, donors want to know that you have a secure payment processing system!</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">What is Payment Fraud?</h3>



<p>Payment fraud is when someone steals a person&#8217;s private information (such as their credit card number) and uses it for false or illegal transactions. This can be done through hacking, or simply by tricking somebody.</p>



<p>You know those suspicious texts from strangers claiming to be the IRS? That&#8217;s an example of a scam where someone&#8217;s trying to commit payment fraud!</p>

<p>Yes, nonprofits can use CashApp. However, there&#8217;s not a huge benefit beyond how quick it is to accept transactions through your business account.</p>



<p>Since CashApp is used for quick transactions between peers, it won&#8217;t help with things like collecting donor information or sending out receipts. Plus, donations aren&#8217;t tax deductible.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Is Venmo Free for Nonprofits?</h3>



<p>Venmo is not free for nonprofits, but it&#8217;s very affordable. There are no processing fees for receiving donations for the first 30 days. After that, Venmo charges a 1.9% + $0.10 fee to receive funds.</p>



<p>If you&#8217;re just starting up and looking to save costs, Venmo could be the way to go!</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Is CashApp or Venmo Better for Nonprofits?</h3>



<p>If you&#8217;re trying to decide between CashApp and Venmo for nonprofits, Venmo is probably the way to go. It&#8217;s familiar to lots of people, is backed by Paypal and now has a Charity Profile option to offer additional support.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Is Paypal or Venmo Better for Nonprofits?</h3>



<p>Using Paypal for nonprofits is a trusted way to go. It&#8217;s well-established and offers detailed reports, but it also asks donors to create a profile before giving. While plenty of people have these accounts, it could be a turn-off for new donors!</p>



<p>Venmo, on the other hand, is great for fast, small transactions. It&#8217;s great for reaching younger donors, but is generally used more often for small transactions between peers.</p>



<p>Choose the online payment processor that fits the kind of donations you expect you&#8217;ll be receiving!</p>
